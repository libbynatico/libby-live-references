**PART A --- PRIOR OCCUPATION: AZ COMMERCIAL DRIVER**

Matthew Herbert has been a licensed commercial driver since December
2004 --- a career spanning 21 years. He holds an AZ licence endorsement,
the highest commercial vehicle classification in Ontario, authorizing
operation of tractor-trailers and the full range of heavy commercial
vehicles.

His commercial driving history includes operation of fully loaded
triaxle dump trucks and other heavy transport vehicles. He has worked
for commercial operators including Larry Bonus (CVOR 031193416). His
CVOR record, licensing history, and commercial driving experience are
documented in the Life Appeal Binder and the Driver Improvement Office
correspondence on file.

Commercial driving was not a transitional or entry-level position for
Matthew Herbert. It was a 21-year skilled trade career representing his
primary occupational identity and income base. There is no comparable
occupation he can transition into without retraining, and the physical
restrictions documented in this section affect the full range of
driving-intensive work --- not only long-haul transport.

**Physical Requirements of AZ Commercial Driving**

The physical demands of AZ commercial driving are not discretionary.
They are regulatory and operational requirements that apply to every
driver on every shift. They include:

> • Sustained seated positioning for extended periods --- federally
> regulated driving hours permit up to 13 hours of driving time per day
> with mandatory rest breaks. A shift routinely involves 8 to 13
> consecutive hours in a seated driving position.
>
> • Lap and shoulder seatbelt use --- mandatory under the Highway
> Traffic Act and federal motor carrier regulations for the full
> duration of operation. The lap belt crosses directly over the lower
> abdomen.
>
> • Physical loading and unloading --- depending on the role, commercial
> drivers are required to perform physical tasks including securing
> loads, operating equipment, and in some contexts manually handling
> freight.
>
> • Reliable and predictable physical function --- a driver operating a
> heavy commercial vehicle must be able to complete a route without
> unscheduled stops for medical reasons. Unpredictable gastrointestinal
> events that require immediate access to facilities are incompatible
> with route-based commercial driving.
>
> • CVOR medical fitness certification --- commercial drivers in Ontario
> are required to hold a valid medical certificate under O. Reg. 340/94
> confirming they meet the physical standards for commercial vehicle
> operation.

Matthew Herbert cannot meet these requirements. The reasons are
documented below.

**PART C --- NARRATIVE: WHY THIS IS PERMANENT AND TOTAL IN THIS
OCCUPATION**

Each restriction documented above is individually significant. Together
they are occupationally conclusive.

Commercial driving does not permit partial performance. A driver either
completes the shift or does not. The vehicle either operates safely or
it does not. The seatbelt is either worn or the driver is in violation
of the Highway Traffic Act. There is no modified duty pathway in AZ
commercial transport. There is no desk version of driving a
tractor-trailer.

The seatbelt restriction alone is sufficient to end a commercial driving
career. It is not a matter of discomfort or preference. The lap belt
crosses directly over an abdomen that has been permanently altered by
the compounding effects of malnutrition-related surgical staple failure,
open wound management, wound VAC therapy, and the resulting permanent
tissue deformity. Sustained pressure from a lap belt in that anatomical
context is not safely tolerable for the duration of a commercial driving
shift.

The seated positioning restriction compounds this. Even absent the
seatbelt issue, the physical demands of 8 to 13 hours in a cab seat ---
vibration, posture loading, pressure on the abdominal and pelvic region
--- are beyond what the patient\'s post-surgical abdominal structure can
sustain.

The ostomy management requirement is the third independent barrier.
Route-based commercial work requires predictable physical function
across a shift. Crohn\'s disease and a permanent ileostomy already
create baseline unpredictability. Post-surgical complications have
worsened that baseline. A driver who must make unscheduled stops for
medical management cannot safely or reliably operate on a commercial
route.

None of these restrictions are expected to resolve. The abdominal
deformity is permanent. The ileostomy is permanent. The post-surgical
structural changes are permanent. The occupational consequence is
therefore permanent.

**PART D --- CAUSATION: THESE RESTRICTIONS ARE DOWNSTREAM OF CMH**

These functional restrictions are not pre-existing. They are not a
natural progression of the patient\'s Crohn\'s disease or ileostomy.
They are the result of a specific sequence of events that originated at
Cambridge Memorial Hospital.

**Pre-admission baseline:** Matthew Herbert held a valid AZ commercial
licence and had 21 years of commercial driving experience. His Crohn\'s
disease and ileostomy were managed conditions. He was employed and
employable as a commercial driver before the CMH admission.

**What CMH produced:** Severe malnutrition --- a 29% weight loss under
supervision --- and prolonged steroid dependence caused by medication
refusal. These were not inevitable complications of his conditions. They
were the result of inadequate inpatient management.

**What HHS added:** The malnutrition and steroid dependence caused
post-surgical staple failure. The authorization failure delayed wound
VAC therapy. The delayed therapy contributed to the extent of the
permanent abdominal tissue deformity.

**The result:** A permanent physical deformity of the abdomen that is
directly incompatible with the physical requirements of AZ commercial
driving. This is not a consequence Matthew Herbert would have
experienced had CMH met its standard of care. It is a consequence of
documented institutional failure.

The causal argument is: but for the CMH failures, there would have been
no malnutrition-driven staple failure. But for the staple failure, there
would have been no open wound requiring VAC therapy. But for the wound
VAC delay, the permanent disfigurement may have been prevented or
substantially reduced. And but for the permanent disfigurement ---
combined with the ongoing ostomy complications --- Matthew Herbert
retains the physical capacity to return to commercial driving.

> *This is the income loss argument in its strongest form: the permanent
> functional restrictions that prevent return to a 21-year occupation
> are directly caused by institutional failures that were preventable.
> This is not a case where a pre-existing condition ended a career. This
> is a case where a hospital\'s failure to provide basic nutritional
> care during a 44-day admission set off a chain of events that ended
> one.*

**PART E --- TBI, AUDITORY PROCESSING DISORDER, AND BARRIERS TO
ALTERNATIVE EMPLOYMENT**

The physical restrictions documented in Parts B and C prevent Matthew
Herbert from returning to commercial driving. This section documents why
those restrictions also foreclose a straightforward transition to
alternative employment --- and why the income loss in this case cannot
be resolved by retraining or redirection to a different occupation.

**Traumatic Brain Injury --- 2001**

Matthew Herbert sustained a traumatic brain injury (TBI) in 2001. The
documented downstream effects include short-term memory impairment and
executive dysfunction. Executive dysfunction affects the cognitive
processes required for planning, task sequencing, error monitoring, and
adapting to new or complex environments. These are precisely the
cognitive demands involved in occupational retraining and transition to
a new field.

Commercial driving was an occupation that Matthew Herbert had mastered
over 21 years. It did not require the kind of novel cognitive processing
that his TBI makes difficult. The physical skills, route knowledge, and
operational procedures were deeply practised and well within his
functional capacity. A new occupation --- particularly any skilled
trade, technical, or knowledge-based role that requires formal
retraining --- places demands on exactly the cognitive domains most
affected by his TBI.

Short-term memory impairment further limits occupational options
requiring the retention and application of new procedures, regulatory
requirements, or client-specific information. This is not a mild or
manageable deficit in the context of competitive employment --- it is a
documented neurological consequence of a 2001 injury that has been
present throughout the patient's adult working life and was accommodated
by his choice of a highly routinized, physically skilled occupation.

**Auditory Processing Disorder (APD)**

Matthew Herbert has a documented Auditory Processing Disorder (APD). APD
is a neurological condition in which the brain has difficulty processing
and interpreting auditory information accurately despite normal
peripheral hearing. It is distinct from hearing loss. The functional
consequences include difficulty understanding speech in noisy
environments, difficulty following multi-step verbal instructions, and
fatigue from sustained auditory processing demands.

In an employment context, APD creates significant barriers to roles that
involve: verbal instruction in training environments, telephone-based
communication, customer-facing service work, team-based environments
with high ambient noise, and any role where accurate real-time auditory
comprehension is operationally required. The majority of entry-level and
mid-skill occupations available to a worker transitioning out of
commercial driving involve one or more of these demands.

**Combined Effect on Alternative Employment Capacity**

The combination of TBI-related executive dysfunction, short-term memory
impairment, APD, permanent physical abdominal restrictions, ongoing
Crohn's disease and ileostomy management requirements, and post-surgical
recovery creates a cumulative barrier to alternative employment that is
substantially greater than any single factor in isolation.

Commercial driving accommodated all of these factors simultaneously. It
was a solitary, routinized, physically skilled occupation that did not
require rapid cognitive adaptation, verbal instruction processing, or
team-based communication. The loss of that occupation does not leave a
range of comparable alternatives. It leaves a labour market that is
substantially inaccessible to a person with this combination of physical
and neurological profile.

A formal vocational assessment and neuropsychological evaluation will be
required to fully quantify the impact on earning capacity. These
assessments are identified in Section 9 as expert retentions for
Siskinds. What this section establishes is the factual and clinical
basis for that assessment: the loss of commercial driving is not a
circumstance from which Matthew Herbert can simply retrain his way out.
