**Medical Basis**

Matthew Herbert sustained a traumatic brain injury (TBI) at age 12 in
2001. His treating physician described this injury as a moderate
concussion with mild brain damage and permanent short-term memory loss.
This has resulted in documented auditory processing disorder (APD) and
executive dysfunction, confirmed across multiple clinical assessments
from childhood through adulthood.

**What This Means in Practice**

APD is not a hearing volume problem. Audiometric testing shows adequate
volume detection. The deficit is in the brain's ability to interpret,
organize, and retain spoken language --- particularly when the
information is complex, delivered quickly, in a noisy environment, or
under cognitive load.

> • He may appear to understand information in the moment but has
> significant difficulty recalling details afterward
>
> • Busy service environments significantly reduce processing capacity
>
> • By the time he reaches a counter after navigating transit and
> managing medical conditions, cognitive capacity may already be
> depleted
>
> • APD is an invisible disability --- do not assume comprehension means
> retention

**Legal Basis**

**AODA Customer Service Standard (O. Reg. 191/11):** Requires government
service providers to communicate with persons with disabilities in a
manner that accounts for their disability.

**Ontario Human Rights Code, ss. 1 and 11:** Prohibits discrimination in
services on the basis of disability. Requires accommodation to the point
of undue hardship.

**Written communication and permission to record are not undue hardships
for any government office.**
