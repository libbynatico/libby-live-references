The offer that followed my accommodation request was Wednesday December

requested as a disability-related accommodation.

> *The Stage 1 denial should be set aside on procedural grounds alone. A

**GROUND 2 --- SUBSTANTIVE: MISAPPLICATION OF ELIGIBILITY CRITERIA**

**The IQ Issue --- Why It Does Not Determine Eligibility**

The Stage 1 decision appears to have treated a Full Scale IQ score of 92
as determinative. SIPDDA does not set a fixed IQ threshold. The
regulation requires \'significantly subaverage intellectual
functioning\' --- a standard that permits consideration of the full
cognitive profile including the documented discrepancy between
structured test performance and real-world functional capacity.

My ABAS-3 adaptive behaviour scores fall below the 1st percentile across
all domains. This is not a borderline result. It reflects a person
functioning at an extremely low adaptive level in every domain assessed
--- daily living skills, social functioning, practical skills ---
despite an average performance IQ in a structured clinical setting. The
discrepancy between these two results is itself clinically significant
and consistent with the cognitive profile of acquired brain injury.

A person with ABAS-3 scores below the 1st percentile in every domain is
not functioning at the level that their IQ score might suggest. The
real-world evidence --- 20+ jobs that could not be sustained, complete
dependence on partner and external systems for daily functioning,
inability to navigate administrative systems without support, reliance
on AI tools to communicate --- is consistent with those adaptive scores,
not with the IQ figure.

**The Age of Onset Issue --- Why the Record Is Incomplete**

The Stage 1 decision states there is insufficient evidence of impairment
before age 18. This finding was made on the basis of records that were
assessed without the in-person meeting I requested --- and therefore
without my opportunity to provide context and supplementary evidence.

What the record does establish:

> • TBI sustained at age 12 (2001). The hospital record cited as showing
> \'no lasting deficits\' reflects findings at the acute discharge stage
> --- not a longitudinal assessment of functional impact in the years
> following. Post-concussive effects, executive dysfunction, and
> auditory processing disorder are frequently underdiagnosed at the time
> of acute injury and emerge or are identified years later.
>
> • Crohn\'s disease onset at age 13. This is a progressive, chronic
> condition with onset before 18 that has permanently affected
> gastrointestinal structure and function, required major surgery, and
> produced ongoing disability. Onset before 18 is documented.
>
> • School records referencing functional difficulties post-TBI exist
> and were not fully reviewed in the Stage 1 process. These records
> would have been available for discussion at the in-person meeting that
> was not provided.

The evidence of pre-18 functional impact is present. It was not
adequately reviewed. A Stage 2 reconsideration with the in-person
accessible meeting I requested is the appropriate mechanism to address
this gap.
