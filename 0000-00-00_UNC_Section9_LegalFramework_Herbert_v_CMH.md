**PART A --- MEDICAL NEGLIGENCE: COMMON LAW FRAMEWORK**

**The Four-Part Negligence Test**

A claim in medical negligence in Ontario requires establishing four
elements: (1) a duty of care owed by the defendant to the plaintiff; (2)
a breach of the standard of care; (3) causation --- the breach caused
the harm; and (4) damages.

The leading authority remains Ter Neuzen v. Korn, \[1995\] 3 SCR 674 and
the standard of care framework in ter Neuzen and Crits v. Sylvester
(1956), 1 DLR (2d) 502 (Ont. CA), affirmed \[1956\] SCR 991. The
standard is that of the reasonably competent practitioner in the
relevant specialty.

**Application to This Case**

**Duty of Care --- CMH:** The duty of care is not in dispute. Cambridge
Memorial Hospital owed Matthew Herbert a duty of care from the moment of
his admission on June 26, 2025 through to discharge on August 8, 2025. A
hospital owes a non-delegable duty of care to patients in its care:
Yepremian v. Scarborough General Hospital (1980), 28 OR (2d) 494 (CA).

**Duty of Care --- HHS/Juravinski:** HHS owed a duty of care in respect
of post-operative management. The surgical team\'s responsibility for
post-operative wound care coordination --- including ensuring Ontario
Health atHome received the required authorization form --- falls within
the scope of that duty.

**Standard of Care --- Nutrition:** The standard of care for an
inpatient with documented Crohn\'s disease and ileostomy requires active
nutritional monitoring and intervention when weight loss reaches
clinically significant thresholds. A 14% weight loss in 13 days is a
clinically critical event requiring urgent response. The failure to
intervene despite the patient\'s formal written notification falls below
the standard of a reasonably competent care team managing a complex
inflammatory bowel disease patient.

**Standard of Care --- Medication:** The Health Care Consent Act, 1996
requires informed consent before treatment is administered. The
patient\'s established medication protocol was refused without
documented clinical justification. No compliant substitute was offered.
This falls below the applicable standard regardless of the clinical
team\'s rationale, which was not documented.

**Standard of Care --- Discharge:** Discharge planning for a complex,
malnourished patient must include an adequate plan for community
management. Discharge to no fixed address without an adequate discharge
plan is not consistent with the standard of care for a patient with
Matthew Herbert\'s documented complexity and nutritional state.

**Standard of Care --- HHS Authorization:** Post-operative care
coordination is within the scope of a surgical team\'s duty. The failure
to provide a required wound care authorization form to the home care
provider is a failure of that coordination duty. It is not a clinical
judgment call --- it is an administrative step that was required and not
taken in a timely manner.

**Causation:** The causal chain is documented in Section 2. The key
causal links are: CMH malnutrition causing surgical staple failure (to
be confirmed by Juravinski operative report); HHS authorization failure
causing wound VAC delay; wound VAC delay contributing to permanent
disfigurement. The but-for test (Athey v. Leonati, \[1996\] 3 SCR 458)
and material contribution analysis (Clements v. Clements, 2012 SCC 32)
are both potentially applicable depending on how the causation analysis
develops.

**Damages:** Documented in Section 8. Physical, permanent,
psychological, financial, and relational harm established across six
categories.

**PART B --- INFORMED CONSENT AND THE HEALTH CARE CONSENT ACT**

The patient invoked the Health Care Consent Act, 1996, S.O. 1996, c. 2,
Sched. A directly and by name in the Formal Medication Refusal Statement
dated July 5, 2025 (Tab 6, Document 1). This invocation during the
admission is significant --- it establishes that the consent framework
was engaged in real time and that the patient was exercising his legal
rights under Ontario statute.

**Relevant Provisions**

**Section 10(1):** A health practitioner who proposes a treatment for a
person shall not administer the treatment, and shall take reasonable
steps to ensure that it is not administered, unless the health
practitioner is of the opinion that the person is capable with respect
to the treatment, and has given consent.

**Section 11(1):** A person is capable with respect to a treatment,
admission to a care facility or a personal assistance service if the
person is able to understand the information that is relevant to making
a decision about the treatment, admission or personal assistance
service, as the case may be, and able to appreciate the reasonably
foreseeable consequences of a decision or lack of decision.

**Section 14(1):** An individual may give or refuse consent to a
treatment at any time.

The patient\'s Formal Medication Refusal Statement explicitly cites
sections 10(1) and 11(1). He asserted his capacity, refused codeine
administration, and placed a hold on Imodium increases --- all within
the framework of the Act. The following morning, the nurse presented
codeine alongside Imodium, contrary to the agreement documented in the
patient\'s own account and the Day 14 letter (Tab 6, Documents 3 and 4).

The failure to honour the patient\'s documented consent refusal --- and
the subsequent administration of a medication the patient had
specifically refused --- engages section 10(1) directly.

Corporate liability through vicarious liability for employed staff and

Secondary defendant. Exposure confined to the post-operative period:

**LIMITATIONS PERIOD --- STRATEGIC NOTE**

The basic limitation period in Ontario is two years from the date the
claim was discovered: Limitations Act, 2002, s. 4 read with s. 5.

The CMH admission ended August 8, 2025. On a simple calculation the
basic limitation period would run to August 8, 2027. However the causal
chain extends beyond the CMH discharge --- the staple failure, wound VAC
delay, and permanent disfigurement all occurred in 2026. The discovery
of the full extent of harm, particularly the permanent disfigurement,
occurred no earlier than March 2026.

The discovery rule in s. 5 means the clock runs from when the plaintiff
knew or ought to have known of the material facts giving rise to the
claim. Given the progressive and compounding nature of the harm here,
Siskinds should assess the most advantageous discovery date argument.

There is no present concern about a limitations issue given the current
date. This note is included for completeness and to ensure the
limitation period is tracked from the outset of the file.
