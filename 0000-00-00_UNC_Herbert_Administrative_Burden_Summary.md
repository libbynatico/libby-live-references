**What a Single Errand Actually Costs This Person**
===================================================

Matthew has no driver's licence. No personal vehicle. He has been
effectively homeless since June 2025 and has lived in a retired
ambulance for most of the last three years, with no fixed address and
unable to drive it because of his suspended licence. After his August
2025 hospital discharge, he spent approximately 30 days at his parents'
home in Fergus when he was too weak to hold himself up and had to be
discharged without homecare to the only available location. A shelter
was not a medically safe option given his ileostomy and wound care needs
--- he was better off in a tent but needed to stay dry, so the ambulance
became the only viable option.

He is currently in Fergus recovering from January 2026 stoma revision
surgery. His recovery has been terrible --- a vertical incision that
split open, a wound cavity, weeks on a wound vac, and ongoing home
nursing visits. Despite all of this, he is still here, still
cooperating, still presenting himself for compassionate reconsideration
through proper channels.

**The geographic isolation is critical:** Fergus has no public transit
connection to Cambridge, where Matthew's health region and most
government offices are located. When he is in Fergus, he cannot do
anything he needs to do in his region.

He relies on city buses, wifi-only connectivity (phone service drops
when data runs out), and a phone battery that does not last a full day.
The Cambridge bus system is especially difficult --- maps are no longer
posted at stops, everything is supposed to be digital, and most buses do
not have WiFi. For someone unfamiliar with the Cambridge routes and
relying on a phone that may be dead, navigating transit is not a minor
inconvenience --- it is a genuine barrier.

**A "simple" errand** --- dropping off a form at a government office ---
typically consumes three-quarters of a day. The bus schedule is limited.
Transfers are required. Wait times are unpredictable. If the office
cannot process the request, the trip produces nothing. If his phone dies
mid-trip, he loses GPS, calls, and documents.

**A medical appointment in Hamilton** requires a full day of travel. The
round trip from Fergus to Juravinski is approximately 150 km. Without a
licence, this requires ODSP-funded medical transportation (costing the
province approximately \$600 per round trip) or assistance from a
private caregiver. That caregiver called the ambulance for Matthew in
June 2025, spent 44 consecutive nights at his CMH bedside, and continues
to provide transportation, advocacy, and daily care coordination --- all
without compensation.

**The physical cost of travel with a prolapsing stoma:** During the
period when Matthew's stoma was gravity-induced prolapsing 6--9 inches
outside his body upon standing, he was still required to take city buses
to attend appointments. He used a walker in public or a rolling suitcase
to carry paperwork. He often missed scheduled days from exhaustion. He
collapsed in public on multiple occasions. Missing a bus meant being
stranded for another 30 minutes. Service providers noted he appeared
more prepared than most homeless clients --- without understanding the
suffering and work it takes for him to do anything at all.

**The cognitive cost is invisible but real.** Every trip requires
planning: which bus, which transfer, what time, which documents to
bring, where the office is, what to say when he arrives. For a person
with TBI-related executive dysfunction, auditory processing disorder,
and chronic cognitive fatigue, each step depletes a finite cognitive
budget. By the time he arrives at the counter, the capacity to process
new verbal information --- like a warning about photo card consequences
--- may already be exhausted.

**The phone and hearing barrier:** Matthew cannot reliably take phone
calls. He was denied hearing aids because his hearing volume is intact
--- but APD means he cannot reliably interpret what he hears. He
self-funded Apple AirPods as a budget hearing device. Government systems
that rely on phone contact are functionally inaccessible to him.

**The Catch-22: Symptoms Blocking Access to Symptom Support**
=============================================================

Matthew qualifies on paper for substantial support through multiple
provincial programs. The programs exist. The eligibility criteria are
met. But accessing those programs requires the very capacities his
disabilities impair:

**DSO:** He applied for Developmental Services Ontario and underwent an
eligibility assessment conducted by Lisa Sedore. The assessment involved
tasks like stacking blocks and drawing shapes under time pressure,
producing a score that did not meet the IQ threshold. However, the
assessment did not account for the compounding effect of his multiple
conditions --- TBI, chronic illness, cognitive fatigue, chronic pain,
and executive dysfunction operating simultaneously. It downplayed
several factors: it noted he arrived on time and appeared organized,
without asking how he got there (his caregiver took time off work,
physically helped him out of bed, and drove him). The assessor told him
he did not need to bring documents, then faulted him for not recalling
details about elementary school teachers or whether he had an IEP. He
was denied. He collapsed on a city bus trying to reach a subsequent DSO
appointment on the day of his bowel obstruction.

**The Cambridge Memorial Discharge: August 7, 2025**
====================================================

After 44 days as an inpatient, with meetings already scheduled for the
following Wednesday with senior hospital staff, Matthew was discharged
approximately two hours after his caregiver had left for a brief weekend
away. No advance notice was given. The dietitian did not know. Home care
had not been set up. His NSWOC had no idea. His nutrition assessment
forms were not provided. His PICC line had only been removed that
afternoon.

He weighed approximately 133 lbs at discharge --- down from 164 lbs at
ER admission. The weight loss had gone unnoticed by staff; he had to
point it out himself after dropping from 164 to 117 lbs during the
admission. He phoned his sister, working hours out of town, to retrieve
him. While waiting, he was told on more than one occasion that security
would be called if he did not leave. He had nowhere to go.
