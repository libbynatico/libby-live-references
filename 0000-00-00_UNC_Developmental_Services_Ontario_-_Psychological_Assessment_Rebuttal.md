***3. Program Criteria and Legislative Requirements***
======================================================

Under MCCSS policy and SIPDDA, eligibility for developmental services
typically requires that an assessment confirm three core elements:
cognitive functioning, adaptive functioning, and age of onset.

### **[Core Eligibility Criteria]{.underline}**

  **Criterion**           **Description**
  ----------------------- -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
  Cognitive functioning   Significant limitations in reasoning, problem-solving, judgment, or memory.
  Adaptive functioning    Significant limitations across conceptual, social, and practical skills.
  Age of onset            Limitations began before age 18 and are expected to continue indefinitely.

SIPDDA section 3(1)-(2) clarifies that the cause of a condition does not
need to be congenital. Any condition originating before age 18 that
results in significant and lifelong limitations in cognitive and
adaptive functioning may qualify.

**Relevant SIPDDA Definitions and Interpretations**
---------------------------------------------------

Below is a summary of key legal concepts and how they were applied, or
misapplied, in my case.

### **[SIPDDA Definitions and Application]{.underline}**

  **Legal term**                      **Plain-language meaning and how it applies in my case**
  ----------------------------------- -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  Developmental disability (s.3)      A developmental disability involves significant limitations in both cognitive and adaptive functioning that began before age 18 and will continue for life. In the assessment, these limitations were documented but then reframed as "medical" or "physical," which is inconsistent with SIPDDA, since medical or acquired causes in childhood are explicitly allowed.
  Cognitive functioning               Cognitive functioning includes reasoning, learning, memory, judgment, and problem-solving. There is no single fixed IQ cut-off in SIPDDA. My meaningful cognitive weaknesses were overshadowed by a focus on a composite FSIQ, even though specific deficits in knowledge, reasoning, and mental flexibility were documented and affect my daily functioning.
  Adaptive functioning                Adaptive functioning includes conceptual, social, and practical skills needed for everyday life. On the ABAS-3, my scores were below the 1st percentile across all domains, indicating severe and pervasive adaptive impairment. Instead of recognizing this as qualifying evidence, the report discounts these findings because medical issues contribute to my limitations.
  Onset before age 18                 Limitations must originate before age 18; the cause can be congenital or acquired (such as brain injury or chronic illness). My traumatic brain injury at age 12 and Crohn's Disease diagnosis at age 13 clearly satisfy the developmental-age onset requirement.
  Expected to continue indefinitely   Limitations are expected to be lifelong or chronic rather than temporary. Both my neurological and medical impairments are ongoing and well documented. They are not short-lived or fully treatable conditions.

**C. Cognitive Functioning -- Satisfied (functionally significant limitations)**

Although my Full Scale IQ was reported as 92 (within the broadly average

***5. Procedural and Interpretive Errors***
===========================================

Several problems in how the assessment was interpreted and applied have
affected the outcome:

1.  **Cause treated as disqualifier.** SIPDDA explicitly allows medical
    > and acquired causes that arise before age 18. The fact that some
    > impairments are linked to Crohn's Disease or traumatic brain
    > injury should not exclude them from being considered under
    > "developmental disability" criteria.

2.  **Overreliance on FSIQ.** The conclusion appears to rely heavily on
    > an averaged Full Scale IQ score rather than the documented
    > functional weaknesses in specific domains and the real-world
    > limitations they create.

3.  **Assumptions based on missing records.** The report suggests there
    > is insufficient evidence of early impairment partly because school
    > records or early childhood documents were not presented. However,
    > these were never requested from me, and the absence of documents
    > does not prove the absence of impairment.

4.  **Understatement of support needed to attend the assessment.** The
    > report comments that I attended the appointment on time and
    > implies this indicates independence. In reality, my partner took
    > time off work, ensured I had my belongings, transported me, and
    > helped me remain oriented. I did not accomplish this
    > independently.

5.  **Discounting ABAS-3 results.** The report uses ABAS-3 examples to
    > describe my functioning but then discounts the diagnostic
    > significance of scores below the 1st percentile because medical
    > issues contribute to my limitations, instead of recognizing that
    > medical and neurological conditions can be part of a qualifying
    > developmental disability profile under SIPDDA.
