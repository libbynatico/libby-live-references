**SECTION 8 -- Assessment Interpretation Errors**

The psychological assessment used in Mr. Herbert's eligibility
determination contained several interpretive and procedural errors that
directly affected the conclusion.

These errors resulted from an incomplete application of the criteria
under Ontario Regulation 276/10 and a failure to incorporate the
required developmental framework.

8.1 Over-Attribution to Adult Traumatic Brain Injury

The assessment attributed the majority of Mr. Herbert's adaptive
functioning impairments to his adult traumatic brain injury (TBI).

This interpretation is inconsistent with both the legislation and the
documented developmental trajectory.

Evidence demonstrates that Mr. Herbert exhibited lifelong difficulties
with memory, organization, comprehension, and executive functioning well
before any adult injury.

The TBI exacerbated pre-existing impairments; it did not create them.

8.2 Failure to Review Developmental and Educational History

A valid developmental disability determination requires evaluation of:

-- School records

-- Childhood functioning

-- Academic progression

-- Lifelong patterns of impairment

The assessor did not request or review Mr. Herbert's school records,
which contain clear and consistent evidence of childhood developmental
impairment.

8.3 Improper Weighting of Adult Presentation

Adaptive functioning was evaluated primarily based on Mr. Herbert's
current adult presentation, without integrating:

-- Developmental origin

-- Historical patterns

-- Academic functioning

-- Habilitative support history

This approach is contrary to O. Reg. 276/10, which requires origin of
impairment before age 18.

8.4 Contradictory Findings Within the Report

The assessment contains internal contradictions, including:

-- Statements of "strong insight" alongside "limited insight"

-- Observations of "independent functioning" alongside "significant
support reliance"

-- Claims of "adequate comprehension" despite documented difficulty with
multi-step instructions

These contradictions were not reconciled, which undermines the
reliability of the conclusions.

8.5 Lack of Integration of Habilitative Support Needs

The assessment did not evaluate lifelong habilitative support needs
despite this being a central eligibility requirement.

Mr. Herbert's history clearly demonstrates a need for assistance
acquiring and applying adaptive skills since childhood.

8.6 Misapplication of Adaptive Functioning Criteria

Adaptive functioning was interpreted too narrowly, focusing on isolated
strengths rather than:

-- Pervasive deficits

-- Functional consistency

-- Real-world performance

-- Documented developmental history

This approach is inconsistent with accepted standards (AAIDD, DSM-5) and
the legislative framework governing developmental disability
assessments.

8.7 Conclusion on Assessment Validity

Because of these errors and omissions, the assessment cannot be
considered a complete or reliable representation of Mr. Herbert's
developmental profile.

As such, its conclusions should not be used as the sole basis for
determining eligibility.
