**The Catch-22: Symptoms Blocking Access to Symptom Support**
=============================================================

Matthew qualifies on paper for substantial support through multiple
provincial programs. The programs exist. The eligibility criteria are
met. But accessing those programs requires the very capacities his
disabilities impair:

**DSO:** He applied for Developmental Services Ontario. He was denied,
in part, because he could not attend the offered in-person assessment
--- because his stoma was prolapsing 6--9 inches outside his body upon
standing, and his TBI-related cognitive fatigue made travel to the
assessment site physically impossible. He literally collapsed on a city
bus attempting to reach a DSO appointment for passport supports. The
symptom the program exists to support was the barrier to accessing the
program.

**ODSP:** Processing required valid government photo ID. He had none.
Obtaining the only available form of ID (the Ontario Photo Card)
cancelled his driver's licence without adequate disclosure. The system
designed to support him created a new barrier.

**Ontario Works Transportation:** His caseworker denied taxi
authorization for a surgical wound care appointment because the taxi
company requires a credit card. Matthew does not have a credit card. The
system's payment infrastructure is inaccessible to the person it's
supposed to serve.

**Driver's Licence Reinstatement:** He paid \$4,939.10 in fines using

**The Cambridge Memorial Discharge: August 7, 2025**
====================================================

This event illustrates what happens when systems do not communicate and
a patient has no advocate present.

After 43 days as an inpatient, with meetings already scheduled for the
following Wednesday with senior hospital staff to discuss ongoing care,
Matthew was discharged approximately two hours after his private
caretaker had left for a brief weekend away. No advance notice was given
to the caretaker. The dietitian did not know he was being discharged.
Home care had not been set up despite being discussed. His NSWOC (ostomy
nurse specialist) had no idea. His nutrition assessment forms were not
provided. His PICC line had only been removed that afternoon.

He weighed approximately 133 lbs at the time of discharge. He had to
phone his sister, who was working several hours out of town, to come
retrieve him. While waiting, he was told on more than one occasion that
security would be called if he did not leave. He had nowhere to go. Home
care was not arranged. He was discharged to nothing.

This is not an isolated failure. This is what happens when a medically
complex person with cognitive impairment has no assigned support worker,
no case manager, and no one to ensure that the systems responsible for
his care actually coordinate with each other.
