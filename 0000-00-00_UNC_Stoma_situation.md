1\. Comprehensive Clinical Observation -- Prolapsed Ileostomy and
Peristomal Complications

The patient presents with a severely prolapsed ileostomy that
demonstrates significant and rapid positional fluctuation. The stoma
frequently extends several inches beyond the abdominal wall when
upright, appearing edematous, congested, and under tension. When the
patient assumes a horizontal position, the stoma retracts partially or
nearly flush with the skin within approximately 15 minutes, indicating a
gravity-dependent mechanical instability rather than a fixed prolapse.

Upon removal of the appliance, the stoma was markedly prolapsed with a
bulbous, lobulated contour and prominent mucosal folds. The exposed
bowel was bright red to deep pink, moist, and visibly swollen,
consistent with edema and venous congestion. The peristomal skin
exhibited significant erythema and maceration with fissuring and
superficial lacerations. These open areas cause intense pain when
effluent contacts the skin, persisting long after cleansing.

After remaining supine for 15 minutes, the stoma retracted
substantially, now lying closer to the skin surface. The tissue
maintained moisture and viability but appeared mildly dusky from recent
congestion. Effluent residue was visible beneath the wafer, and adhesive
integrity appeared partially lost. This demonstrates that appliance seal
failure is inevitable, as no single fitting can accommodate both the
prolapsed and retracted positions without significant leakage risk.

The ongoing mechanical changes result in frequent appliance
dislodgement, uncontrolled leakage, and continuous peristomal skin
exposure to output. This cycle of trauma has produced chronic
dermatitis, open skin tears, and severe pain, creating a substantial
barrier to self-care.

3\. Functional and Psychological Impact

The patient cannot stand or walk for more than several minutes without
the stoma swelling and prolapsing, causing acute pain and mechanical
strain.

A medical abdominal support belt provides no practical assistance in
preventing these events.

He lives in a constant state of fear and anxiety over when the prolapse
will worsen to the point of collapse, blood loss, or syncope.

This condition severely limits mobility, independence, and quality of
life, and meets the definition of a medical crisis requiring surgical
and specialist reassessment.

⸻

4\. Sleep and Safety Concerns

The patient experiences fear and anxiety surrounding sleep due to
repeated nocturnal appliance failure and the risk of rolling onto the
stoma.

He reports waking in effluent-soaked bedding and experiencing episodes
of rolling onto the prolapsed stoma, causing compression, pain, and
swelling.

The risk of vascular compromise or mucosal injury contributes to chronic
hypervigilance, sleep deprivation, and anxiety.

This represents an ongoing medical and psychological crisis.

⸻

5\. Nutritional Fear and Functional Limitation

The patient experiences persistent fear and anxiety around eating, as
meals often precipitate unpredictable stoma behavior leading to
retraction, prolapse, or appliance failure.

He describes each meal as a potential trigger for a medical event,
resulting in avoidance of food and irregular eating patterns.

This poses nutritional risks and contributes to psychological distress.

Integrated gastroenterological, dietary, and mental health interventions
are warranted.

⸻

6\. Sleep Dysregulation, Pain, and Medication Impact

"I sleep to not be in pain."

The patient exhibits profound sleep dysregulation, sleeping 12--16 hours
daily and occasionally missing entire calendar days.

He wakes in significant pain and stiffness, which delays mobility and
impairs medication adherence.

Missed doses result in withdrawal symptoms and rebound pain,
perpetuating a cycle of exhaustion and destabilization.

This complex presentation requires coordinated pain management, sleep
assessment, and case support to stabilize daily functioning.
