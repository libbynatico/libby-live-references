**APPENDIX D -- Medical & Functional Evidence**

**D-1 Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI) Overview**

Mr. Herbert experienced a traumatic brain injury in adulthood.

Medical documentation confirms persistent post-concussive symptoms,
including:

-- Working memory impairment

-- Reduced processing speed

-- Difficulty sustaining attention

-- Cognitive fatigue

-- Difficulty learning new information under stress

These symptoms compound, but do not create, the lifelong developmental
impairments documented in his educational and developmental history.

**D-2 Persistent Post-Concussive Symptoms**

Post-concussive symptoms have remained stable and chronic, including:

-- Impaired attention and concentration

-- Reduced mental stamina

-- Difficulty processing verbal material

-- Slowed cognitive recovery during stress

-- Difficulty multitasking

-- Worsening of pre-existing learning and organizational challenges

These symptoms do not explain Mr. Herbert's developmental impairments
prior to adulthood.

**D-3 Functional Limitations in Daily Living**

Mr. Herbert experiences significant limitations in daily functioning,
including:

Conceptual:

-- Difficulty understanding multi-step instructions

-- Forgetting tasks and steps

-- Impaired reasoning and problem-solving

Social:

-- Misinterpreting communication

-- Difficulty advocating for himself

-- Vulnerability in professional and bureaucratic interactions

Practical:

-- Difficulty maintaining routines

-- Poor organization

-- Trouble managing appointments, forms, and deadlines

-- Difficulty navigating medical and administrative instructions

These functional impairments began in childhood and persist today.

**D-4 Lifelong Habilitative Support Needs**

Mr. Herbert has required habilitative support for:

-- Acquiring adaptive skills

-- Understanding instructions

-- Managing routines

-- Completing tasks

-- Navigating academic, medical, and bureaucratic settings

-- Sequencing daily responsibilities

These supports were required in childhood, adolescence, and adulthood.

The need is lifelong and consistent with developmental disability.

**D-5 Corroborating Historical Evidence**

Additional evidence of lifelong developmental impairment includes:

-- Difficulty interpreting and applying instructions

-- Trouble generalizing skills to new environments

-- Longstanding cognitive fatigue

-- Difficulty completing tasks without external support

-- Documented academic challenges throughout childhood

-- Consistent need for adult assistance to complete basic
responsibilities

This aligns with the definition and criteria of a developmental
disability under Ontario Regulation 276/10.
