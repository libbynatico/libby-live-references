Core Issue: Multi-System Failure
--------------------------------

Matthew Herbert was admitted to CMH in acute distress following
complications of Crohn's disease and a high-output ileostomy. Over 43
days of inpatient care, he lost approximately 47 pounds --- nearly 29%
of his body weight --- while under continuous hospital supervision.

The weight loss was not flagged by the clinical team. The patient
himself identified the deterioration upon emerging from delirium on
approximately Day 11 and began escalating formally from Day 10 onward.
His escalation letters document:

-   14% body weight loss within 13 days, unmonitored and undocumented by
    staff

-   Medication refusals repeatedly overridden or ignored

-   Pain management unchanged from community outpatient script despite
    surgical recovery

-   No nutritional consultation ordered despite rapid weight loss

-   Discharge planning initiated while patient was medically unstable

-   Wrong medication administered to patient (witnessed and documented)

-   PICC line protocol failures (witnessed and documented)

**In January 2026, surgery at Juravinski Hospital revealed a kinked
bowel and hernia --- a structural mechanical cause for the high output
and deterioration that was not identified during 43 days at CMH. This is
the causation anchor: the condition that explains why conservative
management failed and why the patient continued to deteriorate.**

**Date: July 5, 2025 (Day 10) \| Room: B4-226a, Surgical Floor --- CMH**

To Whom It May Concern,

This is a formal documentation of my medication refusals and care
boundaries during my current inpatient stay at Cambridge Memorial
Hospital.

In accordance with the Health Care Consent Act, 1996 (Ontario): Section
10(1) states that no treatment may be administered without informed
consent. Section 11(1) defines a capable person as one who can
understand the relevant information and appreciate the consequences of
their decision.

I am capable of making informed medical decisions and am exercising that
right below.

**1. Refusal of Codeine (Including Tylenol 3)**

I do not consent to the administration of codeine or codeine-containing
medications, including Tylenol 3. I have a documented history with
codeine and do not wish to return to that medication path. As discussed
directly with Dr. Penner, the aim is to support healthy and sustainable
bowel function, not suppress it with opioids. I also have active Crohn's
disease, which may continue to impact both my symptoms and my daily
functioning. No disrespect to Dr. Green, but introducing codeine now
would interfere with accurately assessing digestive function and is not
an acceptable approach for me under any circumstance. Please document
this refusal in my chart and ensure it is respected by all members of
the care team.

**2. Hold on Further Increases to Imodium**

I also do not consent to any increase in Imodium dosage beyond the
current 2 capsules twice daily. This dosage was increased by Dr. Penner
before his departure, and we agreed it would remain at this level until
reassessment early next week. Any further adjustment prior to that
review would be premature and inconsistent with the plan we established
together.

These decisions are informed, intentional, and consistent with my
treatment discussions with Dr. Penner. I remain open to collaborative
care, but these boundaries must be respected until a formal reevaluation
can take place.

**Signed: Matthew Herbert**

**Date: July 8, 2025 (Day 13) \| To: Attending Medical Staff \| CC:

**Subject: Urgent Request for Intervention, Accountability, and

Since being admitted on June 26, 2025, I have experienced serious

I was unable to notice the severity of my own decline until emerging
from a period of delirium. When I became lucid enough to track basic
facts --- my body weight, dosage schedule, meal intake, and cognition

what had happened to my body under your supervision.

The problem is not just the weight loss --- it's the lack of coordinated
response, the lack of escalation, and the assumption that my condition
could be managed with routine vitals, basic medications, and unchanging
scripts.

Despite undergoing surgery and entering a steep state of malnutrition

consultation, no interdisciplinary review, and no effort to tailor
treatment to my current condition.

**Discharge Is Not a Safe or Ethical Option**

The idea that discharge planning would even be on the table while I am
still in this state --- dropping weight, untreated pain, incomplete
recovery --- suggests a systemic failure to monitor, coordinate, and
adapt care to changing conditions.

**Request for Immediate Action:**

-   Urgent nutritional reassessment and escalation to internal medicine

-   Formal pain management consult with a physician

-   Coordinated interdisciplinary team review of my case

-   A written care plan developed and implemented while I remain under
    supervision

-   Documentation of this deterioration trend in my chart

If I walk out of this building weaker, sicker, and less stable than when
I entered, I want the record to show that I named the problem while I
was still inside.

**Signed: Matthew Herbert**

Cora's Incident Log
-------------------

The following incidents were documented by Cora, who was present as a
visitor/support person during the CMH admission. These are transcribed
verbatim from the original document.

**Incident 1: Ativan Denial --- Friday July 18, 2025**

Matt asked for Ativan at 4:30 pm. Abi (nursing student) denied him the
medication, stating she felt he didn't require it. It was listed as an
RPN medication on his chart. Approximately one hour later, she returned
with a crushed pill in powder form and said "Here's your Ativan." Every
other time it was given as a 0.5 mg tablet.

**Incident 2: PICC Line Protocol Failure --- Friday August 1, 2025**

The day nurse did not follow PICC line protocols. She used the straight
push method of flushing instead of the turbulated flush technique. She
used only 1 saline flush (10 ml) instead of the required 2 flushes (20
ml). When reconnecting TPN, she did not sanitize the attachment before
hooking it to the PICC line attachment.

**Incident 3: Wrong Medication Administered --- Saturday August 2, 2025
(overnight, approximately 3:18 AM)**

A covering nurse came in and asked Matt to open his mouth for drops.
Cora woke up and questioned the medication, as Matt had never been
prescribed oral drops. The nurse stated they were for extra secretion.
Cora challenged this, noting the roommate was on drops for secretion,
not Matt. The nurse checked the chart and returned to confirm she had
made an error. She admitted she did not check the wristband because "it
wasn't considered a medicine."

Witness: Cora

*Significance: These incidents document medication administration
errors, protocol violations, and systemic gaps in patient identification
procedures occurring during the same admission in which the patient
experienced 47 lbs of weight loss. The wrong-medication incident, had it
involved a contraindicated substance rather than drops, could have
caused serious harm to an immunocompromised patient.*
