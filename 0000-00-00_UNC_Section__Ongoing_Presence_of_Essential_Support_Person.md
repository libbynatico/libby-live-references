### **Section: Ongoing Presence of Essential Support Person**

I require the ongoing presence of my designated support person, Kellsie
Hnatejko, for the full duration of my hospital stay, including overnight
and extended periods, as frequently as I determine necessary.

This is not a matter of comfort or personal preference---it is a legally
protected accessibility need rooted in both **physical and cognitive
disability**. My conditions include Crohn's disease, an ileostomy,
impaired mobility, and traumatic brain injury--related limitations. As a
result, I am not always able to safely manage personal care, mobility,
or medical decision-making without direct support.

Ms. Hnatejko provides essential, real-time assistance that hospital
staff are not always available---or able---to provide. She supports
ostomy-related tasks, hygiene, positioning, communication, and memory
retention during medical conversations. This support cannot be
compartmentalized to "daytime" or "meetings-only" hours, as my needs are
unpredictable and sometimes urgent.

Under the **Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act (AODA)**:

> "If a person with a disability is accompanied by a support person, the
> provider of goods or services shall ensure that the person is
> permitted to enter the premises with the support person and to have
> access to the support person while on the premises."\
> *(Integrated Accessibility Standards, O. Reg. 191/11, s. 4(1))*

This applies across the entire hospital, including overnight units,
unless a **real and demonstrable risk** can be proven---and even then,
the onus is on the institution to propose **equally effective
alternatives**.

Additionally, the **Ontario Human Rights Code** mandates accommodation
of both physical and mental disabilities, stating:

> "Accommodation is a fundamental and integral part of the right to
> equal treatment. It is not a courtesy or a privilege."\
> *(Policy on Ableism and Discrimination Based on Disability, OHRC,
> 2016)*

Restricting Kellsie's access based on arbitrary timeframes or
institutional norms---especially when her presence actively prevents
harm---is discriminatory and counter to the requirement for
**individualized accommodation**.

Finally, under the **Excellent Care for All Act (ECFAA)**, I have the
right to:

> "Receive care in a manner that is respectful of your needs and values,
> and which supports your ability to make decisions and participate in
> your own care."\
> *(ECFAA, Patients' Bill of Rights)*

That right cannot be fulfilled if I am denied the presence of the one
person actively ensuring that I remain clean, calm, safe, and informed.

I am not requesting a visitor privilege. I am asserting a **legally
protected accommodation** grounded in my disability-related needs. The
presence of my support person is required as often as I determine it to
be necessary, and any attempt to reduce or restrict this must be
considered a denial of accommodation.
