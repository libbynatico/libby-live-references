**1. Scope and Limitations**
============================

This document constitutes an independent review of archival evidence
pertaining to the developmental, cognitive, and adaptive functioning of
Matthew Gordon Herbert. It is not a psychological assessment. The
reviewer has not met with, spoken to, or directly observed the subject.
No psychometric instruments were administered.

The purpose of this review is to determine whether the available
documentary evidence, considered in totality, supports or contradicts
the conclusion reached in the November 14, 2025 psychological assessment
conducted by Lisa Sedore, which found that the subject "does not meet
criteria for a developmental disability" under SIPDDA.

This review applies the same statutory framework: the Services and
Supports to Promote the Social Inclusion of Persons with Developmental
Disabilities Act, 2008, and Ontario Regulation 276/10.

**4. Medical and Clinical Evidence (B-1 through B-65)**
=======================================================

**4.1 --- Traumatic Brain Injury and Cognitive Sequelae**
---------------------------------------------------------

Entry B-19 ("Childhood TBI --- Lasting Cognitive Consequences")
documents that the 2001 head injury resulted in processing speed
deficits, short-term memory impairment, organizational difficulties, and
attentional control problems originating in childhood. Entry B-30
("Childhood TBI --- Persistent Symptoms") confirms verbal processing
difficulty, memory lapses, disorganization, impaired planning, and

These entries independently corroborate the school record trajectory.
The cognitive profile documented in clinical records aligns precisely
with the functional deficits observed by teachers from Grade 2 onward.

**4.2 --- Auditory Processing Disorder**
----------------------------------------

Entries B-6 and B-7 document auditory processing disorder confirmed by
audiological assessment. The subject can detect sound at normal volume
but cannot reliably interpret, organize, or retain spoken language. He
was denied funded hearing aids because the deficit is processing, not
volume. He self-funded Apple AirPods as a compensatory device.

This is directly relevant to the assessment conditions. The November 14,
2025 assessment relied on verbal instructions. The subject's reported
behaviour during the assessment --- nodding and appearing attentive ---
is consistent with lip-reading and effortful processing, not with
reliable comprehension. In clinical practice, apparent engagement during
a structured interaction should not be interpreted as evidence that
information was fully processed and retained, particularly in a subject
with documented APD.

**4.3 --- Crohn's Disease and Physical Comorbidities**
------------------------------------------------------

The subject has lived with Crohn's disease since age 13, including total
proctocolectomy and permanent ileostomy at age 24 (2013), stoma revision
surgery in January 2026, gravity-induced stoma prolapse of 6--9 inches,
chronic malnutrition (weight dropped from 164 lbs to 117 lbs at 6'3"
during a 44-day hospitalization summer 2025), and ongoing biologic
infusion therapy. Monthly ostomy supply costs of \$1100--\$1,400
throughout the period under review.

These physical conditions do not disqualify the subject from DSO
eligibility. Under SIPDDA, the cause of developmental limitations may be
medical or acquired, provided onset was before age 18. Crohn's disease
was diagnosed at age 13. The physical burden compounds the cognitive
burden in a way that the ABAS-3 scores accurately reflect.

**4.4 --- Medication Effects on Assessment Performance**
--------------------------------------------------------

The subject is prescribed Vyvanse for ADHD. Stimulant medication is
specifically designed to improve focus, attention, and task persistence
in structured, low-distraction environments --- exactly the conditions
of a one-on-one psychological assessment in a quiet room.

A medicated subject performing well on timed block-design and
pattern-drawing tasks in optimal conditions does not demonstrate
unmedicated baseline functioning. It demonstrates medicated peak
performance. In clinical practice, the relevant question is not whether
the individual can perform isolated cognitive tasks under ideal
conditions, but whether they can sustain adaptive functioning across the
demands of daily life --- managing an ileostomy, navigating public
transit, processing verbal information in noisy environments,
maintaining employment, meeting deadlines, and coordinating with 18+
government systems simultaneously.

Additionally, the subject reports that the assessment involved no
reading comprehension component. Reading and written comprehension are
areas where the subject reports significant difficulty. The assessment
therefore did not measure one of the subject's most functionally
limiting deficits.

**5. Adaptive Behaviour Assessment (ABAS-3)**
=============================================

The ABAS-3 was completed by Kellsie Hnatejko, the subject's primary
caregiver, who has provided daily unpaid support including
transportation, medical advocacy, meal preparation, personal care
assistance, and administrative navigation for an extended period. She
has direct, sustained, daily knowledge of the subject's functional
capacity.

**Results: Every domain fell in the Extremely Low range, below the 1st
percentile.**

This means the subject functions below 99% of the general population in
every area of adaptive behaviour the instrument measures. The domains
assessed include Communication, Community Use, Functional Academics,
Home Living, Health and Safety, Self-Care, Self-Direction, Social
Skills, and Work.

The prior assessment (Sedore, November 2025) acknowledged these scores
but discounted them because "medical issues contribute to the
limitations." This reasoning reflects a misapplication of the statutory
framework. SIPDDA, s. 3(1)--(2), does not exclude medical contributions
to adaptive limitations. It requires only that the limitations
originated before age 18 and are expected to continue indefinitely. Both
criteria are met here.

The ABAS-3 scores are not anomalous. They are the adult expression of
the same pattern documented since Grade 2. Fifteen years of school
records showing the same deficits in the same domains --- organization,
task completion, independent work, processing, memory --- are
independently corroborated by an adaptive behaviour instrument completed
by a daily observer 20 years later. This level of longitudinal
consistency across multiple independent sources is clinically
significant and should not have been dismissed.

**7. Employment History as Adaptive Evidence**
==============================================

The subject reports holding more than 30 official CRA-recorded
employment positions since age 14. He has often held a full-time and
multiple part-time positions simultaneously. He has worked primarily
night shifts due to a delayed circadian rhythm that makes daytime
functioning unreliable.

This pattern is not evidence of capability. It is evidence of
instability. The subject is not unreliable because he lacks motivation
--- the effort required to hold multiple simultaneous jobs demonstrates
the opposite. He is unreliable because his conditions --- TBI-related
executive dysfunction, Crohn's disease, chronic fatigue, APD, and
cognitive load --- make sustained employment without supports
impossible. The cycling through positions represents compensatory
behaviour: when one position becomes unsustainable, the subject finds
another rather than accessing support systems he does not know how to
navigate.

The subject has stated: "I am a terrible employee. I am not reliable. I
wouldn't hire me." This self-assessment is clinically consistent with
the adaptive profile documented across all other evidence sources.

**8. Presentation Versus Capacity --- The Masking Effect**
==========================================================

A recurring theme across all evidence sources is the discrepancy between
the subject's surface presentation and his underlying functional
capacity. He presents as articulate. He arrives with organized
paperwork. He engages cooperatively. Service providers have noted he
"appears more prepared than most of their homeless clients."

This presentation is the product of three things, none of which
constitute independent capacity:

**1. Caregiver labour:** Kellsie Hnatejko takes time off work,
physically helps the subject out of bed, ensures he has his materials,
drives him to appointments, and waits. The appearance of organization is
her work, not his.

**2. Compensatory effort:** The subject has been effectively
self-reliant since age 18, not because he is capable of independence but
because no support structure existed. He finished high school while
working 40+ hours per week. He taught himself to appear functional
because no one was going to do it for him. This is not evidence of
capacity. It is evidence of the absence of support.

**3. Medication:** Vyvanse provides a temporary cognitive scaffold that
allows the subject to focus during structured, low-distraction
interactions. This does not reflect baseline functioning.

The subject has described his experience as similar to a "frog in
boiling water" --- when you sustain a brain injury as a child and are
then diagnosed with an incurable disease, and you have ongoing memory
loss, the decline is so gradual that you cannot perceive it. You do not
know what you do not know. You do not recognize your own deficits
because you have no frame of reference for what functioning without them
would look like. He has reported that his memory loss can feel like a
form of protection: if he does not recall the trauma and difficulties he
has faced, he can focus on what is in front of him without feeling sorry
for himself. This is a clinically recognizable coping mechanism in
individuals with acquired brain injury and chronic illness, and it
should not be interpreted as evidence that impairment does not exist.

A 90-minute assessment in a controlled room measures the presentation.
It does not measure the capacity. The evidence binder measures the
capacity.

**10.2 --- Significant Limitations in Intellectual Functioning**
----------------------------------------------------------------

**Finding: CRITERION MET**

The prior assessment reported FSIQ of 92. However, SIPDDA does not
specify an IQ cutoff. The composite score masks functionally significant
subtest weaknesses:

> • General knowledge: 9th percentile
>
> • Nonverbal reasoning: 16th percentile
>
> • Switching and inhibition: approximately 37th percentile
>

>
> • APD confirmed by audiology
>
> • ADHD diagnosed, managed on Vyvanse
>
> • No reading comprehension component was included in the assessment
> --- an area of significant reported difficulty

These specific cognitive deficits impair everyday functioning in
reasoning, knowledge acquisition, processing, memory, and cognitive
flexibility. The composite FSIQ does not capture the functional impact
of these domain-specific impairments. Furthermore, the assessment
measured medicated peak performance in optimal conditions, not baseline
daily functioning.

**12. Opinion**
===============

Based on a comprehensive review of 134 documentary entries spanning the
subject's entire life from age 7 through age 37, including school
records, clinical documentation, adaptive behaviour measurement,
professional attestation, and administrative correspondence, it is this
reviewer's opinion that the available evidence supports a finding of
eligibility under SIPDDA.

The prior assessment's conclusion that the subject "does not meet
criteria" was reached without access to the school records, without
accounting for medication effects on assessment performance, without
testing reading comprehension, and after the assessor instructed the
subject not to bring supporting documents. When the full evidentiary
record is considered, all four criteria are met.

The subject's surface presentation --- articulate, organized,
cooperative --- is the product of caregiver labour, stimulant
medication, and two decades of compensatory effort in the absence of any
formal support. It should not be confused with independent functional
capacity. The ABAS-3 scores, the school records, the employment history,
the homelessness, the collateral attestation, and the 18+ government
systems navigated without a single support worker all point to the same
conclusion: this individual requires and has always required
habilitative support. The fact that he did not receive it does not mean
he did not need it.
