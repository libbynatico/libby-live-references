**What a Single Errand Actually Costs This Person**
===================================================

Matthew has no driver's licence. No personal vehicle. He has been
effectively homeless since June 2025 --- living in a retired ambulance
for most of that period, and briefly at his parents' home in Fergus for
approximately 30 days after his August hospital discharge when he was
too weak to hold himself up and had to be discharged without homecare to
the only available location. He is currently in Fergus recovering from
January 2026 stoma revision surgery. A shelter was not a medically safe
option given his ileostomy and wound care needs --- he was better off in
a tent but needed to stay dry, so the retired ambulance became the only
viable option.

**The geographic isolation is critical:** Fergus has no public transit
connection to Cambridge, where Matthew's health region and most
government offices are located. When he is in Fergus, he cannot do
anything he needs to do in his region. He relies on city buses,
wifi-only connectivity (phone service drops when data runs out), and a
phone battery that does not last a full day of navigation.

**A "simple" errand ---** dropping off a form at a government office,
for example --- typically consumes three-quarters of a day. The bus
schedule is limited. Transfers are required. Wait times are
unpredictable. If the office cannot process the request, the trip
produces nothing. If his phone dies mid-trip, he loses GPS navigation,
cannot receive calls, and cannot access digital documents.

**A medical appointment in Hamilton** requires a full day of travel. The
round trip from Fergus to Juravinski Hospital is approximately 150 km.
Without a licence, this requires ODSP-funded medical transportation
(costing the province approximately \$600 per round trip) or assistance
from a private caretaker who has been providing unpaid support
throughout this period. That caretaker called the ambulance for Matthew
in June 2025, spent 43 consecutive nights at his hospital bedside, and
continues to provide transportation, advocacy, and daily care
coordination --- all without compensation.

**The physical cost of travel with a prolapsing stoma:** During the
period when Matthew's stoma was gravity-induced prolapsing 6--9 inches
outside his body upon standing, he was still required to take city buses
around town to attend appointments and beg for help. He was using a
walker in public or a rolling suitcase to carry his paperwork to
meetings. He often missed scheduled days from pure exhaustion. He
collapsed in public on multiple occasions, needing to lie down because
the prolapse was taking the wind out of him. Missing a bus meant being
stranded for another 30 minutes. Service providers have noted he appears
more prepared than most of their homeless clients --- without
understanding the suffering and work it takes for him to do anything at
all.

**The cognitive cost is invisible but real.** Every trip requires
planning: which bus, which transfer, what time, which documents to
bring, where the office is, what to say when he arrives. For a person
with TBI-related executive dysfunction, auditory processing disorder,
and chronic cognitive fatigue, each of these steps depletes a finite
cognitive budget. By the time he arrives at the counter, the capacity to
process new verbal information --- like a warning about photo card
consequences --- may already be exhausted.

**The phone and hearing barrier:** Matthew cannot reliably take phone
calls. He was denied hearing aids because his hearing volume is intact
--- but his auditory processing disorder means he cannot reliably
interpret what he hears, particularly in noisy environments or over the
phone. He does not qualify for funded hearing devices because APD is a
processing issue, not a volume issue. He self-funded Apple AirPods as a
budget hearing aid for in-person and phone interactions. Government
systems that rely on phone contact are functionally inaccessible to him.

**The Catch-22: Symptoms Blocking Access to Symptom Support**
=============================================================

Matthew qualifies on paper for substantial support through multiple
provincial programs. The programs exist. The eligibility criteria are
met. But accessing those programs requires the very capacities his
disabilities impair:

**DSO:** He applied for Developmental Services Ontario and underwent an
eligibility assessment conducted by Lisa Sedore. The assessment involved
tasks like stacking blocks and drawing shapes under time pressure,
producing a score that did not meet the IQ threshold for eligibility.
However, the assessment did not account for the compounding effect of
his multiple conditions --- TBI, chronic illness, cognitive fatigue,
chronic pain, and executive dysfunction operating simultaneously. It
downplayed several factors: for example, it noted that he arrived on
time and appeared organized, without asking how he got there. In
reality, his private caretaker had taken time off work, physically
helped him out of bed, and driven him to the appointment. The assessor
also told him he did not need to bring any documents, then faulted him
in the report for not being able to recall details about his elementary
school teachers or whether he had an IEP. He was denied. He collapsed on
a city bus while trying to reach a subsequent DSO appointment for
passport supports on the day of his bowel obstruction.

**Government Photo ID / CDCP:** In approximately November 2025, Matthew
was required to obtain valid government photo identification. The timing
coincided within the same week with his health card renewal, CDCP
(Canadian Dental Care Program) approval, and tax filings --- suggesting
the photo card was required to process CDCP, not necessarily ODSP.
Regardless of which program triggered it, the result was the same: the
Ontario Photo Card was the only available form of government photo ID,
and obtaining it cancelled his driver's licence without adequate
disclosure. The system designed to support him created a new barrier.

**Ontario Works --- Administrative Disconnect:** Matthew's file was
approved for ODSP effective December 3, but was then suspended over an
"address change" when he had to stay at his parents' home for 24/7
ongoing support and care. The hospital would not proceed with his
January surgery unless he had somewhere to be discharged to ---
discharging into homelessness would set the system up for additional
emergency burden. But the system that was supposed to help him find
housing other than a shelter could not do so, and a shelter was beyond
medically unsafe for him given his ileostomy and wound care needs.
Separately, taxi authorization for a surgical wound care appointment was
denied because the taxi company requires a credit card, which Matthew
does not have. The system's payment infrastructure is inaccessible to
the person it's supposed to serve.

**Driver's Licence Reinstatement:** He paid \$4,939.10 in fines using
every dollar of his first ODSP payment --- before buying a single thing
for his unborn twins. He was then told to restart graduated licensing
from G1.

**The Cambridge Memorial Discharge: August 7, 2025**
====================================================

This event illustrates what happens when systems do not communicate and
a patient has no advocate present.

After 43 days as an inpatient, with meetings already scheduled for the
following Wednesday with senior hospital staff to discuss ongoing care,
Matthew was discharged approximately two hours after his private
caretaker had left for a brief weekend away. No advance notice was given
to the caretaker. The dietitian did not know he was being discharged.
Home care had not been set up despite being discussed. His NSWOC (ostomy
nurse specialist) had no idea. His nutrition assessment forms were not
provided. His PICC line had only been removed that afternoon.

He weighed approximately 133 lbs at the time of discharge --- down from
164 lbs when he entered the ER. The weight loss had gone unnoticed by
staff; he had to point it out himself after dropping from 164 to 123 lbs
during the admission. He had to phone his sister, who was working
several hours out of town, to come retrieve him. While waiting, he was
told on more than one occasion that security would be called if he did
not leave. He had nowhere to go. Home care was not arranged. He was
discharged to nothing.

This is not an isolated failure. This is what happens when a medically
complex person with cognitive impairment has no assigned support worker,
no case manager, and no one to ensure that the systems responsible for
his care actually coordinate with each other.

**Not having a driver's licence is not an inconvenience.** It is a
