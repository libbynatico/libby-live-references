This patient has persistent post-concussive symptoms following traumatic

\- Auditory processing impairment

These impairments are neurological and often invisible.

*[Clinical implications:]{.underline}*

\- Verbal instructions alone are frequently lost or fragmented.

\- The patient may appear calm, articulate, or engaged while not fully
retaining information.

\- Understanding may only emerge after time to process.

\- Distress or confusion may appear hours later rather than during the
interaction.

*[Required accommodations:]{.underline}*

\- Important clinical information must be provided in written form
whenever possible.

\- Verbal information must be paced, repeated, and confirmed.

\- Written summaries of plans, changes, and decisions are essential.

\- The patient may require time after conversations for information to
"settle."

As an accessibility accommodation, the patient may audio record clinical
conversations for personal medical continuity, safety, comprehension,
and future continuity of care and support. These recordings are private
and used to accurately track care information across shifts and
disciplines.

*[Legal Basis for Accommodation]{.underline}*

These needs fall under the Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities
Act (AODA) and the Ontario Human Rights Code. The Integrated
Accessibility Standards Regulation requires public institutions,
including hospitals, to provide information and communication in
accessible formats upon request, taking into account the individual's
disability, unless doing so would cause undue hardship.

Written communication, pacing, and permission to audio record are not
preferences -- they are required accommodations to ensure equal and safe
access to care.
