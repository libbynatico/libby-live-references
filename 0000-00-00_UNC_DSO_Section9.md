**SECTION 9 -- Anticipated Counterarguments and Rebuttals**

Section 9 identifies the most likely counterarguments that may be raised
during the reconsideration process and provides clear, evidence-based
rebuttals grounded in Ontario Regulation 276/10 and the documented
developmental record.

9.1 Counterargument: "The traumatic brain injury explains the
impairments."

Rebuttal: The presence of a traumatic brain injury does not negate a
lifelong developmental disability.

O. Reg. 276/10 requires that impairments originate before age 18, which
is clearly documented in Mr. Herbert's academic and developmental
history.

The TBI worsened pre-existing impairments but did not create them.

9.2 Counterargument: "There is insufficient documentation from
childhood."

Rebuttal: The legislation does not require complete records from early
childhood.

DSO policy permits reconstruction of developmental history using
available evidence---including school records, academic trajectories,
teacher comments, and lifelong patterns of functioning.

Mr. Herbert's educational and functional records clearly establish
developmental origin.

9.3 Counterargument: "Mr. Herbert appears articulate, so he must not be
developmentally impaired."

Rebuttal: Verbal fluency does not equate to adaptive functioning.

Many individuals with developmental disabilities present as articulate
while struggling significantly with reasoning, planning, sequencing,
problem‑solving, and comprehension.

Adaptive functioning---not verbal presentation---is the legal criterion.

9.4 Counterargument: "He shows strengths inconsistent with developmental
disability."

Rebuttal: Developmental disabilities do not eliminate all strengths.

Individuals often have uneven skill profiles, masking behaviours, or
learned compensations.

Eligibility depends on significant limitations in adaptive functioning,
not the absence of any strengths.

9.5 Counterargument: "He functions independently now."

Rebuttal: He does not.

Mr. Herbert requires assistance navigating medical systems, completing
forms, managing schedules, interpreting instructions, and performing
daily tasks.

These are habilitative support needs, not rehabilitative ones, and are
consistent with a developmental disability that has persisted since
childhood.

9.6 Counterargument: "The adult presentation is the primary concern."

Rebuttal: This position conflicts directly with O. Reg. 276/10.

Eligibility requires developmental origin and lifelong impairments.

Adult functioning must be interpreted through a developmental
lens---especially when adult medical conditions exist alongside
longstanding impairments.

9.7 Summary

These rebuttals demonstrate that commonly expected objections are
already addressed by:

-- Legislative requirements

-- Educational records

-- Functional history

-- Habilitative support needs

-- Documented childhood impairments

This section supports a clear, evidence‑based rationale for
reconsideration.
