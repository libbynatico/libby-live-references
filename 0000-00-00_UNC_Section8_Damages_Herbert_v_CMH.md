**SECTION 8 --- DAMAGES**

*Categories of Harm --- June 2025 to Present*

This section documents the damages sustained by Matthew Herbert as a
result of the institutional failures documented in Sections 2 through 7.
Damages are organized into six categories: physical harm, permanent
harm, psychological harm, financial harm, administrative and systemic
harm, and relational harm.

This section is intended to assist Siskinds in developing a complete
damages claim. Quantum figures are not provided here --- that assessment
requires expert retention and is outside the scope of this documentation
package. What this section provides is a structured account of every
category of harm sustained, with supporting documentation references for
each.

> *Note on framing: The damages documented here do not begin and end
> with the CMH admission. They extend through the post-discharge period,
> the HHS surgical complications, the wound VAC delay, and into the
> present. The harm is cumulative, compounding, and in one category ---
> permanent physical disfigurement --- irreversible.*

**DETAILED DAMAGES NARRATIVE**

**1. Physical Harm --- CMH Admission**

Matthew Herbert entered Cambridge Memorial Hospital on June 26, 2025
weighing 164 lbs. He left 44 days later weighing approximately 117 lbs
--- a loss of roughly 47 lbs, nearly 29% of his body weight, under
continuous inpatient supervision. He has Crohn\'s disease and a
permanent ileostomy. His baseline nutritional vulnerability was known to
the admitting team. It was not managed.

By Day 13 the weight loss had reached 14% --- a threshold that in a
patient with his history and complexity required urgent nutritional
intervention. No such intervention was documented as initiated. The
patient raised this formally in writing on July 8, 2025 (Tab 6, Document
2). The clinical team did not provide a documented response.

Throughout the admission the patient experienced uncontrolled pain at
levels he described as 9/10 or above on multiple occasions daily. His
established pain management protocol was refused on Day 9 (Tab 6,
Document 1) without documented clinical justification. No compliant
substitute was provided. Pain management was not reassessed during the
admission. The patient explicitly stated in his Day 14 letter to Dr.
Green (Tab 6, Document 4) that he was restraining himself from
supplementing with his own prescription medications out of goodwill ---
and that this restraint was purely an act of will against persistent
severe pain.

**2. Physical Harm --- Post-Discharge Surgical Complications**

The CMH admission did not end the physical harm. It initiated a chain of
harm that extended into 2026.

Stoma revision surgery at Juravinski Hospital in January 2026 was
complicated by premature staple failure. The treating team documented
malnutrition and prolonged steroid exposure --- both direct consequences
of CMH --- as causative. The result was an open post-surgical wound in a
patient who was already compromised. Wound VAC therapy was required.

The HHS surgical team then failed to provide the wound care
authorization form to Ontario Health atHome in a timely manner. This
administrative failure caused a delay of several weeks before wound VAC
therapy was initiated. During that delay the patient had an open
post-surgical wound that was not being appropriately managed. Ontario
Health atHome nursing records from March 10--11, 2026 document
moisture-associated skin damage, open skin, and inadequate supply
availability during this period.

**3. Permanent Physical Harm --- Abdominal Disfigurement**

The wound has since begun to heal following wound VAC therapy. The wound
VAC has been discontinued. However, the patient has sustained permanent
abdominal tissue deformity --- an irregular, lumpy abdominal contour ---
that is expected to be a lasting physical outcome.

This is not a speculative future harm. It is a present and documented
physical consequence. It is permanent.

Whether the permanent disfigurement is attributable solely to the
original wound failure, or whether the wound VAC delay contributed to
the extent of the scarring and deformity, is a question that requires
expert clinical opinion. What is not in question is that the
disfigurement exists, that it is permanent, and that it traces causally
to the sequence of institutional failures documented in this binder.

The permanent nature of this harm places it in a distinct category from
the other physical damages. It is not something that will resolve with
time or treatment. The patient will carry this physical consequence for
the rest of his life.

**4. Psychological Harm**

The psychological harm begins with the experience of the CMH admission
itself.

For 44 days the patient wrote formal letters documenting his
deterioration --- letters that were ignored. He watched his weight fall.
He was denied medication that was part of his established treatment
plan. He was refused transfer to a facility with the specialist capacity
to treat him. He requested nutritional support and did not receive it.
His partner supplied food to him at her own expense because the
hospital\'s supply was inadequate. A nursing student denied him a
prescribed anxiety medication and returned an hour later with it in an
unexplained altered form. The wrong medication was administered to him
in the middle of the night by a covering nurse who admitted she had not
checked his wristband. A nurse used incorrect technique on his PICC line
--- the line through which his only source of intravenous nutrition was
delivered.

He was then discharged to no fixed address in a malnourished and
medically unstable state after 44 days of this.

The post-discharge period compounded the psychological harm. The patient
navigated homelessness, an ODSP transition, a licence reinstatement
case, a medical negligence file, surgical recovery, wound complications,
supply shortages, and the high-risk twin pregnancy of his partner ---
simultaneously. He did so while managing Crohn\'s disease, a permanent
ileostomy, a TBI, and post-surgical wound care.

He told this advocacy system: \'I would like to be a contributing member
of society if the administrative barriers and gatekeepers would allow me
to.\'

That is not a statement of defeat. It is a statement of a person whose
capacity to contribute has been systematically blocked --- not by his
own choices, but by institutional failures at every level he has
encountered.

**5. Financial and Administrative Harm**

The financial damages include both out-of-pocket costs and income loss.

Out-of-pocket costs include transportation to medical appointments that
OW refused to cover --- including the February 18 wound VAC appointment
denied by caseworker Colleen Brunelle because Red Top Taxi requires a
credit card. They include ostomy supplies purchased personally because
the formal supply chain had not been adequately established. They
include the food and nutritional supplements that Kellsie purchased and
brought to the patient during the CMH admission because the hospital\'s
nutritional support was inadequate.

The income loss is harder to quantify but more significant in its scope.
The CMH admission and discharge to homelessness created the closed
administrative loop documented in the Life Appeal Binder: no fixed
address made it impossible to stabilize ODSP, which made it impossible
to resolve the licence, which made it impossible to return to work as a
driver. The loop was not broken by the patient\'s failure --- it was
created and sustained by the institutional failures that began at CMH.

This patient held an AZ commercial licence and had 21 years of driving
experience before the system-created loop stripped him of it. The income
loss flowing from the administrative consequences of the CMH discharge
is a documented and quantifiable component of the damages in this case.

**6. Relational Harm**

Kellsie Hnatejko is Matthew Herbert\'s partner. She is currently
pregnant with twins, classified as high risk, with biweekly appointments
at McMaster University Medical Centre.

During the CMH admission she attended the hospital regularly and
supplied food to the patient at her own expense because the hospital\'s
nutritional support was inadequate. This is documented in the Day 14
letter to Dr. Green (Tab 6, Document 4) where the patient explicitly
notes this as unacceptable. She was also present during some of the
incidents documented in Cora\'s witness log.

In the post-discharge period she has been the patient\'s primary support
person through surgical recovery, wound care, ODSP navigation, housing
instability, and the medical negligence file --- all while managing a
high-risk pregnancy.

The relational harm is not separately claimed here as a heads of damages
matter --- that assessment is for Siskinds. What is documented is the
reality: a pregnant woman in a high-risk pregnancy has carried a
disproportionate support burden as a direct consequence of the
institutional failures that left her partner malnourished, disfigured,
homeless, and administratively trapped.
