### **EAP REQUEST -- IMPORTANT NOTES FOR PRESCRIBER**

**Vitamin D (Cholecalciferol) 50,000 IU Twice Weekly**

This request is being submitted through the **Ontario Drug Benefit
Exceptional Access Program (EAP)** because the medication and dosing are
**not listed benefits** for this indication.

#### **Why this qualifies for EAP**

-   The patient has **Crohn's disease with permanent ileostomy and
    > chronic malabsorption**

-   Vitamin D deficiency is **documented and persistent**

-   **Over-the-counter supplementation has failed**

-   The prescribed dose (**50,000 IU twice weekly**) represents
    > **replacement therapy**, not routine supplementation

-   No ODB-listed alternative adequately meets this medical need

#### **Critical wording points (please do not change)**

To avoid automatic denial, the following concepts must be clearly
stated:

-   Use **"chronic malabsorption"** (not just "vitamin D deficiency")

-   State that **OTC vitamin D was ineffective**

-   Use **"replacement therapy"**, not "supplement"

-   Indicate **duration as "ongoing"**

-   Avoid short-term language (e.g., "trial," "8--12 weeks")

#### **Common reasons EAP requests are denied**

-   Framing vitamin D as a supplement

-   Not addressing OTC availability

-   Listing duration as temporary

-   Omitting malabsorption or surgical anatomy

The attached justification letter already addresses all EAP criteria and
can be signed in agreement and attached to the EAP request form.

This letter is submitted in support of an Exceptional Access Program

weekly**, for the above-named patient.

Matthew Gordon Herbert has a complex gastrointestinal history, including
Crohn's disease with multiple bowel resections, colectomy, and a
permanent ileostomy. Due to this underlying disease and permanently
altered gastrointestinal anatomy, he has a chronic and ongoing risk of
malabsorption of fat-soluble vitamins, including vitamin D.

The patient has **documented, persistent vitamin D deficiency over
several years**, and prior trials of **over-the-counter vitamin D
supplementation have been ineffective**, placing him at significant risk
for impaired bone mineralization, osteopenia, and osteoporosis in the
context of chronic malabsorption.

Vitamin D therapy in this case is being prescribed as **medically
necessary replacement therapy at prescription strength**, specifically
**50,000 IU twice weekly**. This dosing reflects impaired absorption and
the need for reliable, consistent therapeutic replacement that cannot be
achieved through dietary intake or over-the-counter preparations.

Over-the-counter vitamin D products are not appropriate in this context
due to:

-   unreliable absorption related to intestinal resection and ileostomy

-   inability to ensure consistent therapeutic dosing at the required
    > strength

-   lack of appropriate clinical oversight and monitoring

This medication and dosing are **not listed as a benefit under the
Ontario Drug Benefit Formulary for this indication**, and there is no
listed alternative that adequately meets the patient's medical needs.

Vitamin D replacement is medically necessary to reduce the risk of
deficiency-related complications, including impaired bone mineralization
and progression to osteopenia or osteoporosis. Therapy is prescribed and
monitored as part of ongoing medical and nutritional follow-up.

Given the patient's permanent surgical anatomy and chronic malabsorptive
risk, this requirement is **ongoing rather than short-term**. Coverage
through the Exceptional Access Program is respectfully requested.
