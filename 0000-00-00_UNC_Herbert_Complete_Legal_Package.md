**5. The Photo Card Act Argument**

Photo Card Act s. 13(2) prohibits holding a Photo Card and a "valid
driver's licence" simultaneously. However, O. Reg. 340/94 defines "valid
driver's licence" as one that is "not expired, cancelled or under
suspension." Matthew's licence was SUSPENDED when he obtained the Photo
Card. A suspended licence is not a "valid driver's licence" under the
regulation. Therefore the Photo Card Act's cancellation mechanism may
not apply to his licence at all.

Furthermore, the CVOR pulled six weeks after the Photo Card was obtained
still records the status as SUSPENDED --- not cancelled. The Ministry's
own system did not treat the Photo Card as triggering a cancellation.

**7. AODA and Human Rights Code**

**IASR s. 12 (O. Reg. 191/11):** Government must provide accessible
formats and communication supports. Cannot rely exclusively on methods a
person cannot effectively use.

**Ontario Human Rights Code --- Duty to Accommodate:** Government must
accommodate to the point of undue hardship.

**Undue Hardship (OHRC Guidance):** Only three factors may be
considered: cost, outside funding sources, and health/safety
requirements. Administrative inconvenience or disruption to standard
processes does NOT constitute undue hardship. The onus is on the service
provider to demonstrate undue hardship --- not on the person with a
disability to prove they deserve accommodation.

Verbal-only disclosure of irreversible licensing consequences to a
person with documented auditory processing disorder does not constitute
adequate informed consent. Matthew's clinical records state he "relies
on written summary for clinical accuracy and to bypass cognitive
fatigue."
