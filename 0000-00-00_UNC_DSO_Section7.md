**SECTION 7 -- History of Requiring Habilitative Support**

Under Ontario Regulation 276/10, a key indicator of developmental
disability is a demonstrated history of requiring habilitative support.

Habilitative support refers to assistance required to acquire, maintain,
or apply adaptive skills across conceptual, social, and practical
domains.

This support is distinct from rehabilitative support, which addresses
conditions acquired later in life.

Mr. Herbert has required habilitative support consistently across
childhood, adolescence, and adulthood.

7.1 Habilitative Support in Childhood

During elementary and middle school, Mr. Herbert required:

-- Repeated explanations from teachers

-- Additional time to complete tasks

-- Step-by-step assistance for assignments

-- Adult prompting to stay organized

-- Help understanding classroom expectations

-- Guidance for transitions and new routines

These supports reflect developmental skill gaps rather than behavioural
or environmental issues.

7.2 Habilitative Support in Adolescence

As school demands increased, Mr. Herbert continued to rely heavily on
teacher support. Records and functional history show:

-- Difficulty managing coursework independently

-- Reliance on structured assistance for planning and scheduling

-- Frequent need for clarification of instructions

-- Inability to complete multi-step academic tasks without support

-- Difficulty prioritizing responsibilities

-- Increased executive functioning deficits

These needs directly align with habilitative criteria under the
regulation.

7.3 Habilitative Support in Adulthood

Contrary to expectations of developmental improvement, Mr. Herbert's
adult functioning demonstrates ongoing need for habilitative assistance.
He requires help with:

-- Understanding medical and administrative instructions

-- Completing government forms

-- Organizing appointments and scheduling

-- Managing daily routines

-- Remembering necessary tasks

-- Navigating unfamiliar environments

-- Task sequencing and follow-through

These needs are not rehabilitative responses to adult-onset conditions.

They are extensions of long-standing developmental impairments.

7.4 Consistency of Support Needs Across Life Stages

The persistence of these support needs demonstrates:

-- Lifelong adaptive functioning impairment

-- Inability to internalize routines without external structure

-- Difficulty applying learned skills across contexts

-- Ongoing vulnerability in environments requiring independent adaptive
functioning

These patterns satisfy the requirement for habilitative support history
under O. Reg. 276/10.

7.5 Impact on Daily Living

Without habilitative assistance, Mr. Herbert experiences:

-- Overwhelm and shutdown in complex situations

-- Inability to complete administrative or medical tasks

-- Disorganization and missed deadlines

-- Reduced safety awareness

-- Functional paralysis during stress

This confirms that habilitative support is a fundamental and lifelong
requirement.
