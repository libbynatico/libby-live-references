**1. What This Appeal Is About**
================================

On November 14, 2025, Matthew Herbert underwent a psychological
assessment for DSO eligibility conducted by Lisa Sedore. Despite the
assessor's own findings documenting severe and pervasive functional
limitations, the final conclusion stated that Matthew "does not meet
criteria for a developmental disability."

This appeal demonstrates that the determination was incorrect. It was
based on a narrow interpretation of the assessment data, procedural
errors that excluded critical evidence, and a misapplication of the
statutory criteria under the Services and Supports to Promote the Social
Inclusion of Persons with Developmental Disabilities Act, 2008 (SIPDDA).

**The assessor's own data, when properly applied to the statutory
criteria, supports eligibility. This appeal asks DSO to re-determine
eligibility based on the full evidentiary record.**

**2.2 --- The ABAS-3 Scores Are Below the 1st Percentile in Every Domain --- And Were Dismissed**
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The Adaptive Behavior Assessment System (ABAS-3) was completed by
Kellsie Hnatejko, Matthew's primary caregiver, who has direct daily
knowledge of his functioning. The results were unambiguous:

**Every single domain fell in the Extremely Low range, below the 1st
percentile.**

This means Matthew functions below 99% of the general population in
every area of adaptive behaviour the instrument measures --- including
communication, community use, home living, health and safety, self-care,
self-direction, social skills, and functional academics.

The assessor acknowledged these scores but then **discounted them**
because "medical issues contribute to the limitations." This reasoning
is legally incorrect under SIPDDA.

**SIPDDA, s. 3(1)--(2), explicitly allows medical and acquired causes**
that arise before age 18. Matthew's traumatic brain injury occurred at
age 12. His Crohn's disease was diagnosed at age 13. Both are
developmental-age events. The statute does not require that the cause be
congenital or genetic. It requires that the condition originated before
18 and results in significant lifelong limitations.

**The assessor applied a disqualifier that the statute does not support.
The ABAS-3 scores meet the adaptive functioning threshold. Full stop.**

**3. The Statutory Test and How It Is Met**
===========================================

Under SIPDDA, O. Reg. 276/10, eligibility for developmental services
requires three elements:

  **Criterion**         **Requirement**
  --------------------- -------------------------------------------------------------------------
  **1. Onset**          Significant limitations originated before age 18
  **2. Intellectual**   Significant limitations in intellectual functioning
  **3. Adaptive**       Significant limitations in adaptive functioning across multiple domains

All three must be present, and the condition must be expected to
continue indefinitely. The cause may be congenital or acquired.

**4. What the Assessment Missed**
=================================

**4.1 --- The Compounding Effect of Multiple Conditions**
---------------------------------------------------------

The assessment evaluated Matthew's performance as if each condition
existed in isolation. It did not account for the compounding effect of
TBI, Crohn's disease, chronic pain, cognitive fatigue, APD, executive
dysfunction, ADHD, and medication effects all operating simultaneously.

No person with one of these conditions would present identically to a
person managing all of them at once. The assessment's failure to
consider cumulative burden is a fundamental interpretive error.

trip. This event occurred on the same day as a bowel obstruction.

The symptom the program exists to support was the barrier to accessing
the program. This is the definition of a Catch-22.

Appearing functional in a 90-minute assessment does not mean being
